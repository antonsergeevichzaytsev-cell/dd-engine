# Tailings Intel — Mining Deal-Killer Reference

**Author:** Anton Zaytsev
**Purpose:** Reference for assessing tailings storage facility (TSF) risk in DD. Tailings is the most common deal-killer in mining DD post-Brumadinho (2019). One bad TSF can write off the asset.
**Cross-reference:** Used in §11–12 of `dd_nickel_pgm.md`, §10 of `dd_gold_hydromet.md`, §9–10 of `dd_copper_concentrator.md`.

---

## 1. Why tailings dominates modern DD

Three major catastrophes have reshaped the industry:

- **Mount Polley (Canada, 2014)** — TSF failure, ~25 Mm³ release, established that "geotechnical assumed conservative" is not enough
- **Samarco / Mariana (Brazil, 2015)** — Fundão dam failure, 19 deaths, ~50 Mm³ release, Vale and BHP exposure ~$10 bn+ over a decade
- **Brumadinho (Brazil, 2019)** — Córrego do Feijão TSF failure, 270 deaths, criminal prosecution of executives, fundamentally changed governance expectations

The industry response was the **Global Industry Standard on Tailings Management (GISTM)**, published August 2020 by ICMM, UNEP, and PRI. Now the de facto global standard for tailings DD.

For DD purposes, tailings risk = **probability of failure × consequence of failure × ESG reputational propagation**. The consequence and reputational dimensions have shifted upward materially. A single fatality from a TSF failure can:
- Trigger criminal investigation of directors and executives
- Suspend operating licence indefinitely
- Trigger Equator Principles / IFC PS lender enforcement
- Trigger insurance exclusions
- Force impairment / writeoff of related assets
- Destroy investor relations for years

## 2. GISTM in 60 seconds

Global Industry Standard on Tailings Management — 15 principles, 77 specific requirements. Mandatory for ICMM member companies (most major Western miners). Increasingly required by lenders, investors, insurers regardless of ICMM membership.

**Six topic areas:**

1. **Affected communities** — engagement, free prior informed consent, grievance mechanisms
2. **Integrated knowledge base** — site characterisation, design basis, climate change, seismic
3. **Design, construction, operation, monitoring** — design standards by consequence classification, construction QA/QC, operations, monitoring
4. **Management & governance** — Accountable Executive (AE), Responsible Tailings Facility Engineer (RTFE), Engineer of Record (EoR), Independent Tailings Review Board (ITRB)
5. **Emergency response & long-term recovery** — preparedness, response plans, financial assurance, closure
6. **Public disclosure & access to information** — transparency

**Consequence classification (Annex 1):**

| Class | Description | Approximate design event return periods |
|-------|-------------|-----------------------------------------|
| Low | Limited impact | 1-in-100 year flood, OBE seismic |
| Significant | Localised impact | 1-in-1000 year flood, MDE seismic |
| High | Moderate impact, possible fatalities | 1-in-10,000 year flood, MCE seismic |
| Very High | Likely fatalities, severe environmental | PMF, MCE |
| Extreme | Mass fatalities, catastrophic | PMF, MCE, with conservatism |

**[ABBREVIATIONS]:** OBE = Operating Basis Earthquake; MDE = Maximum Design Earthquake; MCE = Maximum Credible Earthquake; PMF = Probable Maximum Flood

In DD, **consequence classification must be reviewed**. Operators routinely classify too low to reduce design and construction cost. Independent review of consequence classification is a deal-condition check.

## 3. TSF construction methods — risk hierarchy

### 3.1 Upstream construction

- Dam built on top of previously deposited tailings
- Cheapest, fastest to build
- **Highest failure rate globally** — Brumadinho, Samarco/Fundão were upstream
- Many jurisdictions have banned new upstream construction (Brazil, Chile)
- For DD: **upstream TSF is a structural red flag**. Existence does not kill the deal but warrants:
  - Liquefaction susceptibility analysis (static and seismic)
  - Verified water table management
  - Specific GISTM compliance review
  - Decommissioning plan or conversion plan

### 3.2 Centreline construction

- Dam crest built directly above starter dam centreline
- Intermediate risk
- More robust than upstream but less than downstream
- Still in use globally; acceptable in many jurisdictions with conditions

### 3.3 Downstream construction

- Each raise built progressively downstream of previous
- Most expensive in materials and footprint
- Lowest failure rate
- Industry preferred for new construction

### 3.4 Dry-stack / filtered tailings

- Tailings dewatered to <20% moisture, stacked rather than deposited as slurry
- Eliminates dam structure risk almost entirely
- 3–5x higher OpEx than slurry deposition
- 1.5–3x higher CapEx for dewatering plant
- Increasingly required for new permitting
- Existing operations rarely convert (CapEx prohibitive)

**DD signal:** If asset is using upstream construction in a high-seismic / high-consequence setting, this is a P1 issue. If asset is announcing dry-stack conversion, this is positive but check CapEx is realistic.

## 4. Failure modes

### 4.1 Static liquefaction

- Tailings (loose, saturated, contractive) collapse without external trigger
- Mechanism in Brumadinho
- Hard to predict without specific testing (CPTu, cone penetration with pore pressure)
- Mitigation: drainage, densification, conservative loading

### 4.2 Seismic liquefaction

- Tailings liquefy under earthquake loading
- Risk increases with magnitude of design event
- Risk much higher for upstream than downstream
- Mitigation: design for MCE, post-event inspection protocol

### 4.3 Overtopping

- Water rises above dam crest (insufficient freeboard, blocked spillway, extreme rainfall)
- Erodes dam crest, leads to breach
- Mitigation: freeboard adequacy, spillway capacity, design event update for climate change

### 4.4 Foundation failure

- Underlying soil or rock not adequate for dam loading
- Sliding, settlement, piping
- Mitigation: site characterisation depth and quality, monitoring of foundation pore pressure

### 4.5 Internal erosion / piping

- Water seeps through dam, eroding internal fines, eventual collapse
- Often slow but accelerating
- Mitigation: filters, drainage design, instrumentation

### 4.6 Slope instability

- Outer dam slope fails
- Often triggered by rapid raise rate, undrained conditions
- Mitigation: stability analyses, slope angle, drainage

### 4.7 Climate change loading

- Increased rainfall intensity vs. historical design basis
- Increased frequency of extreme events
- Glacier melt feeding TSF catchment
- Mitigation: design event update, drainage system upgrade

## 5. DD review framework

### 5.1 Documents to request

- TSF design basis (most recent)
- Construction records (raises history, materials, QA/QC)
- Operations Manual (OMS)
- Instrumentation data — piezometers, inclinometers, survey monuments, seepage flows (12-month minimum)
- ITRB reports (last 3 years)
- Engineer of Record (EoR) annual reviews and dam safety inspections
- DSI (Dam Safety Inspection) and DSR (Dam Safety Review) reports — frequencies by jurisdiction
- Emergency Response Plan and EPRP (Emergency Preparedness and Response Plan)
- TARP (Trigger Action Response Plan) — what triggers, what action, by whom
- Stability analyses — static, seismic, post-seismic
- Consequence classification documentation
- Closure plan
- Insurance coverage

### 5.2 Site visit observation points

If site visit in scope:
- Inspect dam crest, slopes, abutments
- Inspect spillway (clear, structurally sound, adequate capacity)
- Inspect pond — beach width, water elevation, sediment patterns
- Identify any seepage, ponding, settlement, cracking
- Speak with TSF operators independently from management
- Check instrumentation reading frequency and method
- Look for "near miss" incidents in reporting

### 5.3 Personnel verification

- **Engineer of Record (EoR)** — Who? Qualifications? Independent or in-house?
- **Responsible Tailings Facility Engineer (RTFE)** — Who? Reporting to whom?
- **Accountable Executive (AE)** — Who at corporate level? GISTM requires named executive accountable for tailings.
- **Independent Tailings Review Board (ITRB)** — How many members, who, when last met?

If these roles are not formally appointed and documented, asset is not GISTM-compliant.

## 6. Specific commodity tailings considerations

### 6.1 Acid rock drainage / acid mine drainage (ARD/AMD)

Applies wherever ore or waste rock contains sulphides oxidising in contact with water and oxygen.

**Highest risk:** Sulphide Cu, Cu-Mo, Cu-Ni-Co, Pb-Zn, sulphide Au, U
**Lower risk:** Oxide ores, carbonate-buffered ores
**No risk:** Most aluminium (bauxite tailings), some iron

ARD risk in DD:
- Static testing (NAG, ANC, NP/AP ratio) of tailings and waste rock
- Kinetic testing (humidity cells, column tests) for time-dependent behaviour
- Long-term water quality monitoring
- Liner systems and seepage capture
- Mine closure water treatment perpetual liability

**Anton's signature:** Long-term water treatment liability after closure is one of the most underestimated DD risks for sulphide assets. Some Canadian and US Pb-Zn/Cu-Au mines have ongoing water treatment 50+ years post-closure with cumulative liabilities exceeding original asset NPV. For greenfield sulphide projects, water treatment NPV must be in the financial model — it usually isn't.

### 6.2 Cyanide tailings (gold operations)

- Cyanide destruction in tailings stream (INCO SO₂/air, peroxide, or natural attenuation)
- Cyanide WAD/total in tailings effluent
- ICMC (International Cyanide Management Code) compliance
- Two major historical incidents: Baia Mare (Romania, 2000), Karamken (Russia, 2009)

DD checks:
- ICMC signatory? Recertified?
- Cyanide destruction technology and performance
- Cyanide assay in tails: <50 mg/L WAD typically; <10 mg/L is best practice for new operations
- Bird/wildlife mortality records

### 6.3 Arsenic, antimony, mercury

Common in gold operations, some Cu-Pb-Zn. These are persistent and difficult to fix in tailings.

- Arsenic fixation as ferric arsenate / scorodite — stability requires specific pH conditions
- Mercury volatilisation in roast/POX — air emissions and tailings disposition
- Antimony in tailings: limited regulatory framework, increasing scrutiny

### 6.4 Radioactive elements

- U mining and processing: tailings contain Ra-226, Rn-222 emission, U residue
- Some Cu, Au, REE operations have low-level radioactive tailings
- Regulatory frameworks vary widely; long-term liability significant

### 6.5 Bauxite residue ("red mud") — alumina

- Highly alkaline (pH 11–13)
- Ajka Hungary failure 2010, 10 deaths
- Most Bayer plants store wet; some shift to dry stacking
- Carbonation processes can reduce alkalinity
- Recovery for cement / iron / scandium under research — not commercial at scale

### 6.6 PGM tailings

- Generally low-toxicity (silicate-rich, low sulphide for Bushveld; Norilsk sulphide higher risk)
- Issue is **PGM lost to tails** — economic, not environmental — typically 5–15% of feed PGE reports to tailings
- Re-treatment of historical tailings for PGM is an active opportunity

## 7. Russian/CIS-specific considerations

### 7.1 Regulatory framework

- Russian dam safety regulated under Rostekhnadzor (Федеральная служба по экологическому, технологическому и атомному надзору)
- Classification of "хвостохранилище" — Russian dam safety classifications I, II, III, IV
- Annual inspection regime; episodic deep inspection
- GOST standards on hydraulic structures (ГОСТ Р 22.1.10-2002 and related)
- Generally less stringent than GISTM in design event return periods and ITRB requirements

### 7.2 Notable incidents

- **Karamken (Magadan, 2009)** — Cyanide release after dam breach following extreme rainfall, fish kill in Khasyn River
- **Bashkir copper-sulphate (2020)** — Various incidents at older facilities
- **Sibay (Chelyabinsk Oblast, 2018)** — Underground fire issues separate but sulphide handling concerns

### 7.3 DD implication

Russian assets generally compliant with Russian regulation but may not meet GISTM. Gap analysis required for any asset intended to attract Western capital or LBMA/ICSG/responsible-sourcing-aligned buyers.

Common gaps:
- No formally appointed RTFE/EoR/AE in GISTM sense
- Consequence classification at lower band than GISTM would require
- Design events shorter return period than GISTM
- ITRB not constituted
- Public disclosure limited (Russian law restrictive on facility-level data)

These are addressable but require investment 1–5 years and $5–50M depending on facility complexity. DD should price this gap.

## 8. Closure liability

Tailings closure is often the largest single environmental liability on a mining asset's balance sheet — and often underprovisioned.

### 8.1 Closure cost drivers

- Dam stabilisation / decommissioning to long-term stable state
- Cover (typically 1–3 m engineered cover, vegetation establishment)
- Spillway permanent works
- Water management (potentially perpetual for ARD)
- Site monitoring (decades)
- Land rehabilitation
- Community handover

### 8.2 Order-of-magnitude

- Small Au TSF (10–50 Mm³): $10–50M closure
- Medium Cu TSF (200–500 Mm³): $50–250M closure
- Large sulphide TSF (1+ Bm³): $500M–$2B+ closure, including perpetual water treatment

### 8.3 DD test

- Closure cost estimate exists? Methodology? Vintage?
- Financial assurance (bond, surety, parent guarantee, in-trust funds)?
- For ARD operations: NPV of perpetual water treatment included?
- Closure cost vs. NPV of remaining LOM — does asset have positive NPV after closure?

Red flag: closure cost estimate is "old" (>3 years), based on dry-cover assumption when wet cover would be required, or excludes perpetual water treatment for ARD-generating operations.

## 9. Killer questions for management

- "Who is your Accountable Executive for tailings, and who do they report to?"
- "When was your last ITRB meeting? Who is on the ITRB? Show me the most recent report."
- "What is the consequence classification of your TSFs, and what is the design earthquake / design flood?"
- "What is the failure mode of greatest concern for each TSF, and what is your mitigation?"
- "Show me the last 12 months of piezometer data on each TSF."
- "What is your monitoring frequency on instrumentation, and who reviews it?"
- "Have you had any TARP triggers in the past 24 months, and how were they resolved?"
- "What is your closure cost estimate, when was it last updated, and what financial assurance is in place?"
- "For any upstream-construction TSF, what is the conversion plan and timeline?"
- "What does your insurance coverage look like for tailings catastrophic failure?"

## 10. Red flags

- Upstream construction in high seismic / high consequence area without conversion plan
- ITRB not constituted or hasn't met in 12+ months
- EoR resigned recently / unfilled
- Piezometer data not collected or reviewed monthly
- Spillway blocked, vegetated, or with structural concerns
- Pond water elevation creeping toward crest
- Crack patterns on crest or slopes
- Past TARP triggers not investigated to root cause
- Closure cost estimate >5 years old, or methodology not aligned with modern practice
- Insurance excluding catastrophic tailings or capped at unreasonably low limits
- Management talks about tailings as a regulatory compliance issue, not a strategic risk
- No Accountable Executive named in GISTM sense
- Bypass channels or "emergency drawdown" plans that depend on releasing untreated tailings to environment
