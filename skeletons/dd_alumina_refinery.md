# DD Skeleton — Alumina Refinery (Bayer Process)

**Применимость:** Bayer process alumina refineries from bauxite. Both standalone and integrated upstream of smelter. Refractory-grade vs metallurgical-grade alumina. Specialty aluminas (calcined, tabular, fused).

**Anton domain:** ERG period exposure (Kazakhstan bauxite/alumina), RUSAL alumina supply chain context (Russian and overseas Bayer plants). Medium signature — bridge between upstream bauxite mining and downstream smelter.

**Critical for integrated aluminium DD:** alumina supply is one of two top variables for smelter economics (power being other). Inadequate alumina supply analysis kills smelter DD.

## §2. Bauxite Source

### 2.1 Bauxite types

**Gibbsite (trihydrate Al(OH)₃) — low-temperature Bayer:**
- Australia (Weipa, Gove, Boddington)
- West Africa (Guinea CBG, Sangaredi)
- Brazil (Trombetas, Juruti)
- Easier to refine, lower energy

**Boehmite (AlOOH) — intermediate:**
- Mediterranean basin (Greece, Turkey, France historically)
- Mixed gibbsite-boehmite in many ores

**Diaspore (AlOOH dense) — high-temperature Bayer:**
- China (most Chinese reserves)
- Russia (Northern Urals — Bogoslovsk)
- Higher temperature/pressure required, more energy

### 2.2 Bauxite quality parameters
- **Available alumina (Al₂O₃) %:** target 45%+
- **Reactive silica (SiO₂) %:** key — silica goes to red mud as desilication product (DSP), consumes caustic
- **Total silica:** quartz vs reactive
- **Iron content (Fe₂O₃):** affects red mud volume
- **TiO₂:** can cause issues
- **Organic carbon:** consumes caustic, hard to remove
- **Carbonates:** CO₃ consumes caustic

### 2.3 Bauxite economics
- **Captive bauxite (integrated):** transfer pricing, true cost
- **Purchased bauxite:** FOB price + freight + handling
- **Quality penalties:** silica modulus (Al/SiO₂), other contaminants
- **Logistics:** bauxite is heavy and low value — transport cost critical (Guinea-China shipping, etc.)

### 2.4 Bauxite supply security
- Reserve life at source
- Mining license / concession terms
- Country risk (Guinea coup 2021, Indonesia export bans historically)
- Alternative sources contracted

## §3. Bayer Process — Refinery Operations

### 3.1 Process steps

**1. Bauxite preparation:**
- Crushing, grinding
- Dilution with spent liquor

**2. Digestion:**
- Caustic soda (NaOH) at temperature/pressure
- Low-temperature for gibbsite: ~140-150°C, atmospheric or low pressure
- High-temperature for diaspore: ~250-270°C, 30-40 bar
- Dissolves Al as sodium aluminate
- Insoluble residue (iron, silica DSP, other) goes to red mud

**3. Clarification (settling, washing):**
- Settling tanks (thickeners) with flocculant
- Counter-current washing (multiple stages)
- Red mud washed of caustic, sent to disposal

**4. Precipitation:**
- Pregnant liquor cooled
- Seed (aluminium hydroxide) added
- Al(OH)₃ crystals grow over hours
- Yield = % of dissolved Al precipitated

**5. Calcination:**
- Al(OH)₃ → Al₂O₃ + 3H₂O at ~950-1,000°C
- Rotary kiln or stationary calciner (CFB)
- Specific energy: 2.7-3.2 GJ/t alumina for modern stationary calciners; older rotary 3.5-4.5 GJ/t

**6. Storage and shipping:**
- Smelter-grade alumina (SGA): bulk handling, conveyor/silo
- Specialty alumina: bagged or controlled handling

### 3.2 Key refinery KPIs

| Parameter | Industry range | Red flag |
|-----------|----------------|----------|
| Specific energy consumption | 9.0-13.5 GJ/t alumina | >14: legacy plant, inefficient |
| Caustic consumption (NaOH) | 50-120 kg/t alumina | >150: high silica bauxite or losses |
| Red mud generation | 0.7-2.0 t/t alumina | Depends on bauxite, not improvable |
| Bauxite consumption | 2.0-2.7 t/t alumina | >3: low-grade bauxite |
| Steam consumption | 1.5-3.5 t/t alumina | High: heat exchanger fouling |
| Refinery yield (Al recovery) | 88-95% | <88%: liquor losses, low precipitation |
| Throughput vs nameplate | ±5% | -10%: bottleneck |

### 3.3 Caustic management
- Caustic is largest non-bauxite operating cost
- Soda losses: red mud (entrained, in DSP), evaporation, spills
- Causticization (recovering Na from Na₂CO₃): some plants use lime, some import additional NaOH

### 3.4 Heat integration
- Steam network — high-pressure to medium-pressure to low-pressure cascading
- Heat exchangers (Bayer is extremely heat-integration intensive)
- Co-generation potential

### 3.5 Calciner type and energy
- **Rotary kiln** (older, less efficient, 3.5-4.5 GJ/t)
- **Stationary calciner (CFB):** Outotec/Outotec, Vereinigte Aluminium-Werke licensed, 2.7-3.5 GJ/t
- **Gas suspension calciner:** Lurgi, similar

### 3.6 Refractory and equipment lifecycle
- Digester corrosion (high T, caustic)
- Heat exchanger fouling (silica DSP scale)
- Calciner refractory campaign life

## §4. Red Mud

### 4.1 Red mud disposal — major issue

**Disposal types:**
- **Wet disposal (lagoon):** legacy, environmental risk
- **Dry disposal (filter press, dry stacking):** modern, less land
- **Stacking with cover:** intermediate
- **Sea disposal:** historic (e.g., Stade, Hungary), now banned in most jurisdictions

**Critical incidents:**
- **Ajka, Hungary 2010:** red mud dam failure, 10 deaths, 35 km²contaminated, $1B+ damages
- **Stade, Germany historic deep sea disposal:** banned
- Various wet lagoon overflows

### 4.2 Red mud chemistry
- High pH (12-13) from residual caustic
- Iron-rich (often 40-60% Fe₂O₃)
- Some valuable: scandium, gallium, REE in trace
- Recovery economics: studied for decades, rarely commercial

### 4.3 Red mud reuse research
- Cement additive
- Iron recovery (back-end DRI processes)
- Soil amendment (limited, controlled)
- Most still goes to disposal

### 4.4 Storage and closure
- Lagoon stability (esp. legacy wet)
- Groundwater monitoring
- Long-term aftercare

**Killer question:** What is the red mud disposal method? When is current storage capacity exhausted? What is plan for next stage? Hazardous classification of red mud in jurisdiction?

## §5. Bauxite Residue and DSP

### 5.1 DSP (desilication product)
- Sodium-aluminum-silicate phases
- Forms during digestion
- Consumes caustic (significant cost)
- Goes to red mud

### 5.2 Silica modulus
- A/S ratio = Available alumina / Reactive silica
- >10 = good bauxite
- 5-10 = acceptable
- <5 = problematic, high caustic consumption

## §6. ESG / HSE

### 6.1 Red mud — as above, dominant ESG issue

### 6.2 Air emissions
- Calciner stack: NOx, SOx, particulates
- Fugitive dust (bauxite handling, alumina handling)
- HF (very low for refinery — main HF concern is smelter)

### 6.3 Water
- Process water (high recycled)
- Cooling water
- Wastewater treatment (caustic neutralization)

### 6.4 Carbon footprint
- Scope 1: fuel for calcination, digestion heating, power generation
- Scope 2: purchased electricity
- Total carbon intensity: 0.8-2.0 tCO₂/t alumina (varies hugely by fuel mix)
- CBAM exposure: alumina is in CBAM scope as precursor to aluminium

### 6.5 Worker safety
- Caustic burns (major hazard)
- High-temperature steam
- Calciner work environment
- LTIFR benchmark: 1.0-3.0 for modern alumina refineries

## §7. Power, Water, Infrastructure

### 7.1 Power
- Cogen typical (steam + power from same fuel)
- Grid backup
- Fuel: gas, oil, coal (worst for emissions)

### 7.2 Water
- High requirement
- Source: river, ocean (rare), groundwater

### 7.3 Logistics
- Bauxite inbound (heavy, low-value): port capacity, conveyor
- Alumina outbound: bulk handling, marine if export
- Caustic inbound: rail/road
