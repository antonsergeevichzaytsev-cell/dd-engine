## §2. Resource & Reserves

### 2.1 Deposit types

**Porphyry copper** (most common, large tonnage low grade):
- Chile (Escondida, Codelco assets, Collahuasi, Los Bronces)
- Peru (Antamina, Las Bambas, Cerro Verde, Toquepala)
- USA (Bingham Canyon, Morenci)
- Mongolia (Oyu Tolgoi)
- Indonesia (Grasberg)
- Cu grade typically 0.3-1.5%
- Often with Mo, Au, Ag credits

**Skarn copper:** Cu-Au-Mo skarn (Antamina type, Sarcheshmeh)

**Sediment-hosted (Central African Copperbelt, Kupferschiefer Poland):** different processing typically

**SVHMS (Sediment-hosted, e.g., White Pine MI, Polish Kupferschiefer)**

**VMS (Volcanogenic Massive Sulphide):** Cu-Zn-Pb, often small but rich

**Manto/Vein style:** Mexican copper, Cobre Pacífico

**Tailings reprocessing:** secondary copper (some operations)

### 2.2 Grade-tonnage profile
- Resource curve (M&I tonnage vs cut-off Cu%)
- Spatial continuity
- Geological complexity (multiple intrusions, fault offsets, supergene enrichment zones)

### 2.3 By-products
- **Molybdenum:** in porphyry typically 0.01-0.05% Mo; significant credit when present
- **Gold:** in porphyry/skarn typically 0.1-0.5 g/t; major credit if higher
- **Silver:** 5-50 g/t typical
- **Rhenium:** in some porphyry (Cu-Mo); high value but small quantities
- **Cobalt:** in African Copperbelt
- **Zinc, lead:** in polymetallic

### 2.4 Supergene enrichment zone
- Above primary sulphide, oxide cap and supergene Cu sulphide enrichment
- Higher grade typically
- Suitable for SXEW (oxides) or sulphide flotation (chalcocite)
- Often depleted early in mine life — verify life remaining

### 2.5 Resource classification
- Same JORC/NI 43-101 rigour as Ni
- For Russian/CIS assets: ГКЗ B/C₁ → Indicated typical
- For Mongolian assets (e.g., Oyu Tolgoi): JORC standard
- For Chilean: Codigo Minero similar to JORC

### 2.6 NSR cut-off and reasonable prospects of economic extraction
- NSR formula: Cu% × Cu recovery × Cu payable × (Cu_price - selling cost) + by-products
- Cut-off at NSR equivalent to operating cost
- Long-term Cu price assumption critical — $3.50-4.50/lb common for FS

## §3. Mining

### 3.1 Open pit (most common for porphyry)

**Key parameters:**
- Pit size and ultimate slope angles (geotech)
- Stripping ratio (LOM and current — current usually higher in early years)
- Bench height (15-30m typical)
- Haul road grade and width
- Mining fleet: shovel size (P&H 4100, CAT 7495), truck size (Komatsu 930E, CAT 797F, 250-400 ton)
- Drill: rotary blast hole

**For very large porphyries (Escondida, Bingham Canyon):**
- In-pit crusher and conveyor system (IPCC)
- Saving haul truck fleet, fuel consumption

**Underground (selective high-grade or block cave for deeper):**
- Block caving (Bingham Canyon UG, El Teniente, Oyu Tolgoi UG, Grasberg DOZ)
- Sublevel caving
- High-tonnage typically 30,000-100,000 tpd UG

### 3.2 Mining cost benchmarks
- Surface mining: $1.5-4.0/t total material moved (waste + ore)
- Block cave: $5-12/t ore
- Sublevel cave: $8-15/t ore
- Cut-and-fill (rare for Cu): $25-50/t ore

### 3.3 Geotechnical
- Pit slope monitoring (radar, prisms, surveying)
- Stability history
- Recent failures or warnings

### 3.4 Deferred mining capex
- Same red flags as Ni
- Block cave development meters vs production schedule critical
- Pit pushback capex deferred

## §4. Mineral Processing

### 4.1 Comminution

**Standard porphyry flowsheet:**
- Primary crushing (gyratory) → SAG/Ball mill → Flotation
- For large operations: HPGR (high-pressure grinding rolls) gaining adoption
- Specific energy: 12-22 kWh/t for porphyry, higher for hard ore

**Grinding:**
- SAG mill: 30-40 ft diameter for large operations, 15-25 MW motor
- Ball mill: 25-30 ft typical
- P80 typically 150-250 μm (coarser than Au-Ag because Cu liberation generally easier)

**Hardness drift:**
- Many porphyries get harder at depth (transitioning from supergene to primary sulphide)
- Throughput drops if mill not upgraded
- Verify ore hardness in current mine plan vs as-designed

### 4.2 Flotation

**Bulk Cu (or Cu-Mo) flotation:**
- Roughers
- Scavengers
- Cleaners (2-3 stages)
- Cu concentrate typically 25-30% Cu

**Cu-Mo separation:**
- If Mo present and economical, separate from Cu after bulk
- NaSH (sodium hydrosulphide) depresses Cu, floats Mo
- Mo recovery typically 60-80% (technically challenging)

**Reagents:**
- Collectors: SIBX, PAX (xanthates), dithiophosphates, mercaptans
- Depressants: lime, cyanide (small amounts for sulphide depression)
- Frothers: MIBC, polyglycols

### 4.3 Concentrator KPIs

| Parameter | Industry range | Red flag |
|-----------|----------------|----------|
| Throughput vs design | ±5% | -10%: bottleneck |
| Cu recovery to concentrate | 84-92% | <84%: oxidized ore, liberation, or process |
| Cu concentrate grade | 24-30% | <22%: penalty exposure, dilution |
| Mo recovery (if applicable) | 60-80% | <60%: low priority, valuable lost |
| Au recovery (in Cu conc.) | 60-85% | <60%: free Au not captured by Cu flotation |
| Ag recovery | 60-85% | <60%: low priority |
| Penalty element (As) in conc | <0.2% | >0.5%: penalty material |
| Reagent cost $/t ore | $1.0-2.5 | High: oxidation, gangue mineralogy |
| Energy kWh/t | 25-45 | >45: hardness or efficiency issue |

### 4.4 Tailings
- Tailings dam — same critical assessment as Ni and Au
- Cu tailings often acid-generating (sulphide-bearing)
- ARD management
- Tailings dam construction type (upstream/downstream/centerline/dry stack)
- Recent major incidents: Mount Polley 2014 (Cu)

### 4.5 SXEW for oxide/supergene ores

**Process:**
1. Heap leach with H₂SO₄ — pregnant solution
2. Solvent extraction (PLS → loaded organic → strip)
3. Electrowinning — Cu cathode

**KPIs:**
- Leach recovery (varies by ore type, 65-90%)
- Acid consumption (kg H₂SO₄/t ore)
- SX organic loss
- EW cathode current efficiency (88-93%)
- Cathode quality LME Grade A

**Modern issues:**
- Aging SXEW assets in Chile, Peru
- Sulphate buildup
- Resin/extractant degradation

## §8. Operating Costs

### 8.1 C1 cash cost benchmarks

**Cu C1 cost curve (2024-2025):**
- 1st quartile: <$1.20/lb Cu (high-grade with by-products, e.g., Escondida historically)
- Median: $1.80-2.20/lb Cu
- 4th quartile: >$2.80/lb Cu (low-grade or stressed assets)

**By-product credit dependency:**
- Cu-Au assets (e.g., Grasberg): negative or very low C1 with Au credit
- Cu-Mo (e.g., Bingham historically): low C1 with Mo credit
- Pure Cu: standard cost position

### 8.2 Cost structure

| Component | % C1 typical |
|-----------|---------------|
| Mining | 30-45% |
| Processing | 20-30% |
| Smelting/refining (TC/RC) | 8-15% |
| G&A | 5-10% |
| Logistics | 5-12% |
| Royalties | 3-8% |

### 8.3 TC/RC dynamics

**TC (treatment charge):** smelter charges concentrate producer to convert to cathode
**RC (refining charge):** refining cost

- Benchmark TC/RC negotiated annually (China large negotiation typically sets tone)
- Spot TC/RC volatile based on concentrate market balance
- Long-term contract pricing
- High TC = concentrate oversupply; Low TC = smelter overcapacity (or concentrate scarcity)

### 8.4 By-product credits — critical
- Mo credit, Au credit, Ag credit can be 30-60% of revenue for byproduct-rich operations
- Realized prices and pricing terms

## §16. Red Flags Checklist

- [ ] Cu recovery <84% to concentrate
- [ ] C1 cost in 4th quartile structurally (not just current dip)
- [ ] Reserve replacement <80% (declining LOM)
- [ ] Concentrate As >0.5% (smelter penalties)
- [ ] Tailings dam upstream in seismic/wet zone
- [ ] No ITRB or last review >18 months
- [ ] Water rights insufficient for LOM
- [ ] Community/Indigenous unresolved opposition
- [ ] Mining fleet average age >15 years without replacement plan
- [ ] Throughput dropping vs design
- [ ] Sustaining capex <60% of depreciation
- [ ] TC/RC structure adverse (long-term commitments at unfavourable terms)
- [ ] By-product credit dependency (>40% of NSR from Mo/Au) without diversification
- [ ] Major customer concentration
- [ ] Block cave development behind production schedule (UG)
- [ ] Pit slope warnings or recent failures
- [ ] HSE deterioration

## §17. Recommendation Logic

### PROCEED:
- Cost position 1st or 2nd quartile
- Resource adequacy 10+ year LOM at proven/probable
- Water and community license secure
- Tailings management aligned with industry best practice
- HSE track record acceptable
- Sustaining capex aligned with depreciation

### PROCEED WITH CONDITIONS:
- Adjustment for [specific finding]
- Escrow for [water rights, community, tailings, environmental]
- R&W on [reserves, concentrate quality, off-take]
- Post-close 100-day [tailings audit, community engagement, capex plan]

### DO NOT PROCEED:
- SDN ownership chain
- Active community/Indigenous opposition without resolution path
- Tailings dam categorized as critical with no remediation funding
- Water license at risk
- Resource fraud indicators
- Imminent regulatory action
