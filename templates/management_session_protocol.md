# Management Session Protocol

**Author:** Anton Zaytsev
**Purpose:** Operational playbook for running management interview sessions during DD. This is where most of the value is extracted — or lost. Documents have written reality; management sessions reveal lived reality. Anton's competitive edge over junior associates is in this room.
**When used:** Phase 3 of `methodology/dd_workflow.md`. Typically week 2–3 of a Standard DD, week 3–5 of Deep DD.

---

## 1. The strategic purpose of the management session

Three goals, in order:

1. **Verify and challenge** what was disclosed in documents. Where management's verbal answer contradicts written disclosure, one is wrong — surface it.
2. **Extract what is NOT in the data room.** Operational reality, near-misses, internal disagreements, recent decisions not yet documented.
3. **Test the team.** Are they competent? Honest? Aligned? Can they execute what they claim?

A management session is **not** an information transfer from target to client. It is structured interrogation under the discipline of professional courtesy.

**Anton's principle:** every answer creates a follow-up question. Never accept the first answer. Probe at least one level deeper on every material topic.

## 2. Pre-session preparation

### 2.1 Mandatory pre-session checklist (12–24 hours before session)

- [ ] All document review complete OR all questions tagged "session-required"
- [ ] Public research complete (sanctions screening, news, peer benchmarks)
- [ ] Issues log up to date with all flagged inconsistencies
- [ ] Killer question list finalised — sequenced, time-boxed
- [ ] Attendee list confirmed from target side — names, titles, areas of responsibility
- [ ] Roles agreed client-side — Anton leads, client observes, specialist (if any) covers specific area
- [ ] Logistics confirmed — video platform, recording consent process, language
- [ ] Confidentiality framework refreshed — NDA scope, what is in/out of scope to discuss

### 2.2 The killer question list

Target structure (Standard DD, 90-minute session, single sitting):

| Block | Time | Content | # KQs |
|-------|------|---------|-------|
| Opening | 5 min | Agenda, ground rules, recording consent | 0 |
| Context | 10 min | Open question to target on strategy / context | 1 |
| Resources / reserves | 15 min | Drill data, classification, recovery test work | 4–6 |
| Operations / process | 20 min | Recovery, throughput, equipment condition, KPIs | 5–8 |
| Capital projects (if applicable) | 15 min | FEL maturity, CapEx confidence, execution | 3–5 |
| ESG / tailings / HSE | 10 min | TSF, environmental, social, safety record | 3–5 |
| Commercial / off-take | 5 min | Customers, pricing, sanctions exposure | 2–3 |
| Governance / team | 5 min | Owner team, succession, change management | 2–3 |
| Open close | 5 min | "What should we be asking that we're not?" | 0 |
| Close | 5 min | Next steps, document follow-ups | 0 |

For Deep DD with multiple sessions, expand each block into its own session.

### 2.3 Anton's prep ritual

Night before, read in this order:
1. Issues log — top 15 issues
2. Killer question list — sequence and time-box
3. Most recent year's management accounts (financial reality check)
4. Last LME / metal price chart (market reality check)
5. Top 3 peer comparables (benchmark reality check)
6. CV of target's PD/COO/Technical Director if available (who am I sitting with)

Result: walking into the session knowing more about the target than the target expects.

## 3. Opening — first 5 minutes

### 3.1 Tone

Professional, courteous, factual. Not adversarial. Not friendly. Anton is a technical advisor doing a structured job, not a hostile auditor and not a sales counterpart.

### 3.2 Specific opening

> Good [morning / afternoon]. Thank you for making the time. I'm Anton Zaytsev, working with [Client] on the technical due diligence on [Asset]. With me is [Client representative] in observer capacity. Today we have a 90-minute structured session covering [list of topics]. The objective is to clarify items from the data room and to understand operational specifics that aren't documented. I'll lead with specific questions; we may go deep on some items.
>
> For the recording — do I have your consent to record this session for note-taking purposes, with the recording shared only with [Client] under our existing NDA? [Wait for confirmation.] Thank you.
>
> Three ground rules from my side. First, if a question is outside what you're authorised to discuss, please say so and we'll come back to it. Second, if you need to verify something rather than guess, please say so — I prefer a verified follow-up over an unverified answer in the room. Third, if anything I say is wrong factually, please correct me immediately.
>
> Shall we start?

### 3.3 Recording consent — critical legal point

- Always obtain explicit verbal consent on recording, captured in the recording itself
- If consent refused, take detailed real-time notes (consider second note-taker on client side)
- Russian, EU, US recording laws differ — default to two-party consent regardless
- Recording is for note-taking aid, not for distribution or evidence

## 4. Question discipline — how to ask

### 4.1 Question types

**Specific factual** (verify something documented):
> The 2024 production report shows recovery of 84.3% for Q3. The 2023 same quarter was 86.7%. What drove the 2.4 percentage-point decline?

**Open exploratory** (find unknowns):
> Walk me through the three biggest operational issues you've had to deal with in the last 12 months — what they were, what you did, how they were resolved.

**Hypothetical** (test thinking quality):
> If metal price dropped 25% from today's level for 18 months, what specific actions would you take on this asset, in what order, and what would it cost?

**Comparative** (test peer awareness):
> How does your unit C1 cost position versus [named comparable asset / general industry quartile]? What's the structural reason for the gap?

**Calibrated specificity** (test honesty):
> On your CapEx contingency — you've stated 12%. What specific risks went into that number, and what would the contingency be if you re-built it bottom-up today?

### 4.2 Follow-up discipline

Anton's rule: **never accept the first answer to a material question.** Probe with one of these:

- "Help me understand the underlying number — where does it come from?"
- "What evidence supports that?"
- "What would change your view?"
- "When did you last verify that?"
- "Who has independent visibility on that other than [the answering manager]?"
- "What does the data look like — can you walk me through the actual chart?"

If the answer is vague or evasive, the **second** answer reveals more than the first. The **third** reveals the truth.

### 4.3 What NOT to do

- ❌ Multi-part questions ("How do you handle X, Y, and Z, and what about A?") — target picks the easy one to answer
- ❌ Leading questions ("Would you agree that the recovery is below benchmark?") — interview becomes about your assertion, not their reality
- ❌ Speaking longer than the answer requires — every minute Anton talks is a minute the target doesn't have to talk
- ❌ Reacting visibly to answers — neutral face, even if answer is alarming
- ❌ Tipping the next question — let the answer surprise the room
- ❌ Premature conclusions — never say "so the picture is clear" mid-session
- ❌ Asking permission ("If I could just ask...") — Anton is doing his job, not begging
- ❌ Filling silence — if target pauses, let them pause. Often they fill it with material information.

## 5. Specific high-yield questions by topic

### 5.1 Resources & reserves

- "Show me the resource model parameters — variogram, search ellipse, block size — and how they relate to drill spacing. Last update?"
- "What is the year-on-year reconciliation between block model and mined production for the last 24 months? Where do the differences come from?"
- "If I commissioned an independent QP to re-estimate, what is the probability they come within ±5% of your numbers? What would they likely change?"
- "What is the largest single drill hole impact on your resource — i.e. if you removed the top drill hole, how does the resource change?"
- "Show me the QA/QC dashboard — blanks, duplicates, CRMs — for the most recent campaign. What was the failure rate, and what was the response?"
- "What's classified as Inferred and what's the mining plan dependency on Inferred material in years 1–5? In years 6–10?"

### 5.2 Operations & process

- "Recovery has been [stated %] year-to-date. What is the variance month to month, and what drives the variance?"
- "What is the longest unscheduled downtime in the past 12 months, what caused it, and what was the lost-production cost?"
- "Show me your maintenance backlog — what's deferred, for how long, at what risk?"
- "If I asked your three top operators what the single biggest operational problem is right now, what would they say?"
- "What's your metal accounting tolerance month-to-month at Level 3? When did you last fail to balance, and what was the cause?"
- "What is the difference between your nameplate throughput and your sustained actual? Where is the bottleneck?"
- "Walk me through your real reagent consumption versus design — by reagent, with variance and trend."

### 5.3 Capital projects

- "Who is the project director? What is their daily time allocation to this project?"
- "Show me your project organisation chart. How many of these positions are filled today versus required at this stage?"
- "Did you produce an owner-side independent cost estimate? What was the variance to the FEED estimate? How was it reconciled?"
- "Walk me through your top 5 risks on this project and what dollar amount they contribute to contingency."
- "What is the critical path activity, what is the float, and what event would push first-metal by 6 months?"
- "Of your last three CapEx projects above $100M, what was final cost versus sanction? What did the owner team learn each time?"
- "When does the contingency drawdown profile go above the physical progress curve? What is your protocol when it does?"

### 5.4 ESG / Tailings / HSE

- "Who is the Accountable Executive for tailings, in the GISTM sense? When did they last visit each TSF?"
- "When did your ITRB last meet? Who is on it? Can I see the most recent report?"
- "What is the consequence classification of each TSF, and how was it set?"
- "Have you had any TARP triggers in the past 24 months? What were they, and what was the response?"
- "What is your closure cost estimate, when was it last updated, what methodology, what financial assurance is in place?"
- "Walk me through your LTIFR by quarter for the past 3 years. Where are the spikes, and what was the root cause?"
- "Has anyone died on this asset in the last 5 years? Show me each investigation report and the closure status of corrective actions."
- "What does your insurance cover for catastrophic tailings failure?"
- "Where is your community engagement on a [Indigenous / Local communities] front — show me the engagement log."

### 5.5 Commercial / Markets / Off-take

- "Show me your top 10 customers by revenue, the contracts, and the pricing structures."
- "What is your current realised price versus LME / LBMA / index? What's the discount structure?"
- "Which contracts have sanctions clauses? Which have force majeure clauses tied to political events?"
- "What is your hedging policy and current hedge book?"
- "Where is your concentrate / refined metal physically going — origin point, destination port, ultimate buyer?"

### 5.6 Governance / Team / Sanctions (handle with care)

- "Walk me through ownership to natural persons. How is each layer documented?"
- "What ownership changes have occurred since 2022, and what was the rationale for each?"
- "Are any of your beneficial owners on any sanctions list — OFAC, EU, UK, Canada, Australia? Who has done that screening, when?"
- "If a US person were involved in a transaction with this asset, what would the compliance posture be?"
- "Where is your bank account, in what currency, and what payment routes are in regular use?"
- "What is the average tenure of your top 10 technical and operational managers? Recent departures?"
- "Show me the management succession plan."

## 6. Reading the room — signal vs noise

Beyond the literal answers, watch for:

### 6.1 Verbal signals

- **Speed of answer**: too fast (rehearsed) or too slow (uncomfortable)
- **Specificity**: precise numbers with context vs. round numbers or hedging
- **Consistency**: same answer same way over time vs. drift across the session
- **Volunteering**: do they give context unprompted, or only answer the literal question
- **Caveats**: "in general", "broadly", "approximately" used often = limited factual confidence

### 6.2 Non-verbal signals (video calls)

- Eye contact pattern when answering specific questions
- Glance to off-camera person before answering certain questions
- Body posture shifts
- Audio cues (typing, papers, off-camera conversation)

### 6.3 Group dynamics

- Who answers each question? Does the right person answer (based on title)?
- Who never speaks? Why?
- Does the CEO override technical answers? Bad sign.
- Does the technical director defer to CEO on technical questions? Bad sign — wrong person in charge of technical reality.
- Interruptions — who interrupts whom? Suggests internal hierarchy/disagreement.

### 6.4 Question avoidance patterns

- **Reframing**: "What you're really asking is..." — they want to answer a different question
- **Generalising**: specific question gets industry-level answer — they don't have specific data
- **Personalising**: "I joined the company in [year], so prior to that..." — they're disclaiming knowledge of something material
- **Bureaucratising**: "Let me check with [function] and come back to you" — buying time, or admitting they don't know
- **Counterattacking**: "Why are you asking that specifically?" — defensive, suggests sensitivity

Anton's response to any of the above: **note it, do not call it out in session, follow up in writing**.

---

## 7. Time discipline

Each question must have a target time. Stay within ±50% of target. If a question is taking 3x its budget, either pause it ("Let me come back to this with additional detail offline") or compress the rest of the agenda.

Never run over 105 minutes without explicit re-contract. Cognitive quality on both sides drops after 90 minutes.

**Better to leave 3 KQs for written follow-up than rush the session.**

---

## 8. The "open close"

Last 5–10 minutes — Anton's signature element. After all structured KQs done:

> Before we close — what should I be asking you that I haven't asked? What would be the most important thing for me to know about this asset that we haven't covered today?

This is often where the most valuable disclosure happens. Target's natural impulse is to fill the open space with what they think matters most. Sometimes this is:
- A coming announcement (new contract, new permit, new risk)
- An internal disagreement (e.g., between technical and commercial leadership)
- A near-miss not captured in documents
- A strategic redirection
- An emerging risk no one has yet pulled together

If they say "nothing — you've covered it all", note it. Means either truly nothing material, or they're disciplined.

---

## 9. Closing

Always close with:

1. **Confirm follow-up items** — list them aloud, target confirms each. Both sides know what's pending.
2. **Confirm next steps** — site visit dates, follow-up session if needed, document requests, response deadlines.
3. **Confirm timeline** — when will Anton's draft DD be ready, what is target's review window.
4. **Thank, end.** No social conversation at the end of a structured session. Sets the wrong tone.

## 10. Post-session within 24 hours

### 10.1 Session notes

Structured by KQ, capturing for each:
- Question asked (verbatim)
- Person who answered (name, title)
- Answer summary (paraphrased, not verbatim)
- Quality of answer: **Strong / Partial / Weak / Evasive / Refused**
- Anton's interpretation (separate column from the answer itself)
- Follow-up action required (Y/N, and what)

### 10.2 New issues to issues log

Any new fact, inconsistency, or risk surfaced → added to master issues log with timestamp.

### 10.3 Updates to risk register

Risks raised in session integrated into risk register with updated likelihood / impact / mitigation.

### 10.4 Follow-up email to target

Within 24 hours, send:
- Summary of session (high-level)
- Follow-up document requests (specific, with deadline)
- Open questions for written response

Do not send your interpretations. Do not signal findings. Stay factual on the email.

### 10.5 Internal debrief with client

15-min call with client lead post-session:
- Top 3 things that emerged
- Material risk changes
- Whether additional sessions are needed
- Whether site visit is more or less critical based on session

## 11. Specific session formats

### 11.1 Standard DD — single 90-minute session

Use template in §2.2.

### 11.2 Deep DD — multiple sessions

Typical structure across 3–5 weeks:
- Session 1 (90 min): Geology, resources, mine plan — with geology and mining management
- Session 2 (90 min): Process, plant, equipment, metal accounting — with metallurgy and operations management
- Session 3 (60–90 min): Capital projects, FEL, execution — with project director
- Session 4 (60 min): ESG, tailings, HSE, community — with HSE management
- Session 5 (60 min): Commercial, off-take, sanctions, governance — with CEO/CFO

Each session focused, each session producing structured notes feeding into integrated DD.

### 11.3 Site visit-combined session

When site visit is in scope:
- Day 1 morning: Opening management session (60 min) with site GM + functional leads
- Day 1 afternoon: Plant walk-through, structured area-by-area
- Day 2: Targeted area sessions (concentrator manager, tailings supervisor, etc.) on-site
- Day 2 evening: Operator/supervisor interviews (separately from senior management — different perspective)
- Day 3 morning: Closing session, clarify findings

### 11.4 Hostile or restrictive session

Sometimes target restricts access (limited time, limited people, refusal to discuss specific topics). Anton's options:

1. **Adjust scope**: explicitly document the restriction in DD report; flag finding-confidence is lower as a result.
2. **Escalate**: ask client to push for better access; lower-tier mandates often respond.
3. **Walk**: if restriction is severe and material, recommend client not proceed. DD that can't be done properly = no DD.

Never lower interrogation standards because the target makes it difficult. The difficulty itself is a finding.

## 12. Recording management

- Recording stored on client-controlled system, not Anton's personal drive
- Encrypted at rest
- Retention as per NDA — typically 3–7 years
- Access log if multiple people on client side
- Transcription via secure tool (not public-cloud generic LLMs)
- Recording NOT shared back to target — it's client's evidence

---

## 13. Language considerations

### 13.1 Russian/CIS clients with Russian-speaking targets

- Conduct in Russian. Translate notes for English-speaking client team afterward.
- Anton's signature in this segment: full operational vocabulary in Russian, gives target no comfort that "the foreigner doesn't really understand" — Anton does, and they know it.

### 13.2 English-speaking client with English-speaking target

- Conduct in English.
- For CIS/Russian-speaking management who claim English fluency but show strain: switch to Russian for technical specifics, English for governance/commercial. They will be more accurate in Russian on technical matters; this is honest and improves DD quality.

### 13.3 Mixed-language target team

- Anton conducts in whichever language target is more comfortable; provides translation to client team after.
- Never accept "interpreter" routing where target speaks Russian → interpreter speaks English. Interpreter shaves nuance. Anton speaks directly.
