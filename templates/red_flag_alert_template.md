# Red Flag Alert Template — Mid-DD Escalation to Client

**Author:** Anton Zaytsev
**Purpose:** Standardised format for escalating deal-killer or major findings to the client mid-engagement, without waiting for the final DD report. Used when DD uncovers something that may change the client's transaction posture before DD is complete.
**Timing:** Typically Week 1–3 of engagement when serious red flags surface from document review, public research, or first management session.

---

## When to use this template

**Use it when:**

1. **Deal-killer found** — finding that, in your professional judgment, should cause the client to halt or radically restructure the transaction.
2. **Material adverse change to deal economics** — finding that shifts valuation by ≥10% of deal value, or shifts business case from positive to negative NPV.
3. **Compliance or sanctions concern** — finding that creates legal exposure for the client, must escalate to client legal counsel.
4. **Time-sensitive opportunity** — finding that requires immediate negotiation move (e.g. discovery that creates leverage with vendor) before deal moves further.
5. **DD scope inadequacy** — discovery that the agreed scope cannot answer a question that has now become critical; need to renegotiate scope or accept residual risk.

**Do NOT use it for:**

- Routine findings that go in the final report
- Anton's own opinions without sufficient evidence base
- Findings that are inconvenient but not material
- Anything where you are uncertain enough that the situation warrants more work before alert

The bar is high. A red flag alert is a serious communication. Anton's credibility is on the line each time.

---

## Format

Subject line discipline: prefix with `[DD RED FLAG]` and the deal/asset name so it doesn't get lost in the client's inbox.

---

## TEMPLATE

```
TO: [Primary client contact — typically DD coordinator + decision-maker]
CC: [Client legal counsel if compliance / sanctions related]
FROM: Anton Zaytsev
DATE: [Date]
SUBJECT: [DD RED FLAG] [Asset Name] — [One-line summary of finding]
PRIORITY: [Urgent / High / Standard]

[Client name],

In the course of the technical due diligence on [Asset], I have identified a finding that I believe warrants immediate attention before the engagement continues, separate from the final report.

## What I found

[2–4 sentences. Specific, factual. No hedging. No padding. Cite the source — document, statement, public record. Quantify where possible.]

[Example structure:]
- The fact: [What it is]
- The source: [Where it comes from, with reference]
- The verification: [What additional verification has been done so far, or that more is needed]

## Why I am raising it now

[1–3 sentences. Why this can't wait until the final report. Typical reasons:]
- The finding is material enough to change deal posture
- Time-sensitive — opportunity may close
- Verification requires action by the client (e.g., legal review, specialist input)
- Continued DD on adjacent topics depends on this being resolved first

## What this means for the transaction

[3–5 specific implications, each in plain language.]

1. **Valuation impact:** [Specific, quantified. E.g. "If verified, this finding implies CapEx is understated by $40–60M, equivalent to ~12% of deal value."]
2. **Risk allocation impact:** [E.g. "May require specific reps and warranties, or escrow of $X for Y period."]
3. **Process impact:** [E.g. "Recommend specialist sanctions counsel be engaged before next management session."]
4. **Scope impact:** [E.g. "Recommend extending DD scope to include independent resource review."]
5. **Timeline impact:** [E.g. "Resolution likely requires 2–3 additional weeks beyond original timeline."]

## What I recommend now

[Clear next-action recommendation. Avoid soft language.]

- [Action 1, with owner — Anton or client]
- [Action 2, with owner]
- [Action 3, with owner]

Specifically, before further DD work continues on [adjacent topic], I recommend [decision required from client].

## What I am NOT saying

[Discipline statement to prevent over-reading the alert.]

I am NOT saying:
- The transaction should be abandoned [unless that IS the recommendation]
- The vendor is acting in bad faith [unless evidence supports]
- The finding is verified beyond what is documented above
- This constitutes [legal / compliance / valuation] advice — those opinions require specialist counsel

I AM saying:
- The finding is material enough to escalate now rather than wait for the final report
- The client should make an informed decision on next steps with this finding in hand
- I am available immediately for a call to discuss

## Suggested call

I propose a 30-minute call to discuss within the next [24–72] hours. Please indicate your availability or have your team coordinate with mine.

Regards,
Anton Zaytsev
[Phone] [Email]
```

## Filling out the template — discipline

### "What I found"

Must be:
- **Specific** — names, numbers, dates, document references
- **Sourced** — readers must be able to trace it back to verifiable evidence
- **Factual** — not yet interpreted into "this means X"; that comes in next sections

Bad: "The CapEx looks understated."
Good: "The vendor's AACE Class 3 CapEx estimate of $480M, dated Q2 2024, was produced by [FEED Contractor] without independent owner-side parallel estimate. My back-of-envelope benchmark of the equipment list against three recent comparable projects suggests CapEx of $540–580M is more likely, equivalent to 12–20% above sanctioned."

### "Why I am raising it now"

Avoid being self-important. The reason must be operational, not "this is a big deal":

Bad: "This is critical to the deal."
Good: "The next management session is scheduled in 4 days, and if I am to test this finding effectively I need the client's agreement on whether to surface it in that session or in a separate workstream."

### "What this means"

Quantify everything quantifiable. Vague is worse than wrong-but-bounded.

Bad: "Could affect valuation."
Good: "If verified at the upper end of my range, NPV at base-case price deck would decline by $80–120M from vendor's representation, implying deal value should be re-set at $X."

### "What I recommend now"

Action-oriented. Owner specified.

Bad: "We need to think about this."
Good: "Client legal counsel to opine on sanctions exposure within 5 working days. Anton to suspend DD work on [adjacent topic] until decision received. Client to indicate whether to proceed with next management session on schedule."

### "What I am NOT saying"

This section is unusual but critical. Senior decision-makers can over-read alerts. Pre-empt the over-read.

A red flag alert is **not**:
- A recommendation to abandon the transaction (unless explicit)
- A legal or compliance opinion (always say this if applicable)
- A final view (it's mid-DD; verification may change the picture)
- An expression of personal opinion (it's a professional finding based on evidence)

## Sample completed alert (illustrative)

```
TO: Mikhail Petrov, Investment Director, [Client Fund]
CC: Sergey Volkov, General Counsel, [Client Fund]
FROM: Anton Zaytsev
DATE: 15 March 2026
SUBJECT: [DD RED FLAG] Asset Alpha — Beneficial ownership change post-2022 requires sanctions counsel review
PRIORITY: Urgent

Mikhail,

In the course of technical due diligence on Asset Alpha, I have identified a finding that warrants immediate attention before the engagement continues.

## What I found

The data room indicates that 35% of the equity in Vendor Holdings was transferred in November 2023 from Mr. X (a Cyprus-domiciled natural person) to Ms. Y (a Russian Federation natural person). Public records (OpenCorporates, accessed 14 March 2026) confirm the registration of this transfer. The Vendor's representations in the data room ("Memorandum on Ownership", page 4) describe the transfer as "arm's-length sale at fair value". However:

- Ms. Y is identified in EU consolidated sanctions list entry # [redacted for this template], current as of February 2026
- Mr. X is not on any current sanctions list per my screening
- The transfer occurred 8 months after Ms. Y's sanction designation
- The "fair value" was paid via [structure described in data room] which is unusual for a Western-style arm's-length transaction
- The transfer was not disclosed in the Vendor's sanctions representation in the Information Memorandum

I have not independently screened these individuals — that is the role of legal counsel. I am surfacing the factual record as identified from data room and public sources.

## Why I am raising it now

The next management session with Vendor's CEO is scheduled for 19 March. If this finding is not addressed before that session, the topic will need to come up in management Q&A, which signals the issue to Vendor before client position is set.

## What this means for the transaction

1. **Sanctions exposure:** EU and likely UK sanctions exposure for client entities. Requires immediate specialist sanctions counsel review.
2. **Deal viability:** Depending on counsel view, may render transaction non-viable for client's EU-domiciled fund vehicle.
3. **Negotiation posture:** If transaction can proceed, Vendor's earlier non-disclosure is a major reps & warranties point.
4. **Timeline:** Resolution likely 5–10 working days for legal review; DD work on adjacent topics can continue in parallel.

## What I recommend now

- [Client] to engage Latham / Linklaters / Cleary (or current panel) sanctions counsel immediately
- Hold management session on 19 March on agenda but defer governance / ownership topics until counsel opines
- Anton to continue DD on operational topics in parallel
- Reconvene client decision call once counsel opinion received

## What I am NOT saying

I am NOT saying:
- The transaction must be abandoned — counsel may find structural solution
- Vendor is acting in bad faith — could be inadvertent non-disclosure, requires investigation
- The finding is verified beyond what is documented in the data room and public records above
- This constitutes legal advice — counsel must opine

I AM saying:
- The finding is material to the client's compliance posture
- It cannot wait for the final DD report
- Specific actions by the client are needed in the next 5 working days

## Suggested call

I propose a 30-minute call within 24 hours. I am available today after 14:00 MSK or tomorrow morning. Please coordinate.

Regards,
Anton Zaytsev
+7 [...] | anton.sergeevich.zaytsev@gmail.com
```

## What happens after the alert

### Client response patterns

**Best case:** Client acknowledges, takes action, thanks Anton for early surfacing. DD continues with adjusted scope or pace. This is the relationship-building outcome.

**Mid case:** Client asks clarifying questions, may want to verify with own team or external counsel, may delay decision. Anton's job is to support the verification without pushing for a specific outcome.

**Difficult case:** Client minimises the finding, pressures Anton to "include it in the final report later". Anton's response: document the conversation in writing ("Per our call of [date], understood the client's position is to defer this finding to the final report. I have noted the alert and the client's election to defer. The finding will be presented in the final report unchanged."), continue DD, and ensure final report fully captures the finding regardless. Anton's name is on the report — preserving record integrity is non-negotiable.

**Worst case:** Client instructs Anton to omit the finding. Anton's response: decline. Anton's professional independence cannot be overridden by client preference on material findings. Risk losing the engagement; preserve professional record. This is rare but it has happened in the industry and is a known career inflection point.

### Anton's own record-keeping

For every red flag alert sent:
- Save alert text in engagement folder with timestamp
- Save client response (email or call notes)
- Save subsequent actions and outcomes
- Document this sequence in lessons_learned post-engagement

This is Anton's professional protection. Independent advisor's reputation is built on cases where alerts were sent and outcomes were proven correct over time. It's also protection if a deal goes bad and post-mortem questions arise about who knew what when.
